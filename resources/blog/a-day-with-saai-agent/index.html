A store owner's day with saai agent — from the 08:00 briefing to the 21:00 close

The morning briefing on the way in, waste-vs-stockout calls before the order deadline, congestion checks at the lunch peak, and the close-out. Nine scenes from a convenience-store owner's day, told through measured saai agent responses.

The conversations below are a demo scenario reconstructed from saai agent responses measured in a real production environment (a convenience store). Figures are specific to that store and period, and vary by store conditions.

The hard part of running a store is that there is too much to watch. Sales, visitors, waste, ordering, displays, congestion, equipment — each has a dashboard, but turning them into what to do today was always the owner's job. saai agent flips that order: ask, and you get the answer with an action plan. Here is a convenience-store owner's day in nine scenes.

08:00 — Arrive, morning briefing

Walk in, open the agent first. "Give me the morning briefing."

The last seven days averaged ₩3.32M a day, well up on the prior week — but visitors fell 29.3%. Revenue and footfall are diverging, and the briefing says so. Which products dipped and which are newly growing, where waste runs high (the rice-ball line, up to 50%), and which shelf move looks most promising (activating the dead zone, up to about ₩680K a month) — all in one pass.

What makes it different from ordinary BI: every item in the briefing is an entrance to the next question.

08:10 — An unexpected answer to "why did it drop?"

The briefing flagged one beverage down 53%. "Why did this drop so hard?"

The agent's answer is not what you expect: "It didn't actually drop." Average sales over the last seven days are up 37.5% versus the prior two weeks; no anomaly pattern, no waste. But orders are at zero — so when the stock runs down, it really will fall. The prescription isn't "respond"; it's "don't stop ordering."

Correcting a wrong premise with data, then following through with a predictive suggestion — cause over surface numbers.

08:20 — Waste and stockouts, both, before the order deadline

The hardest call before the 10 a.m. order deadline: what to cut, what to add.

"Why was yesterday's waste so high?" — mostly over-ordering; one rice-ball SKU took in six, sold one, wasted five. "Any A-grade items running low?" — the best-selling spicy-tuna rice ball keeps bottoming out at 0–2 units, so nudge it up instead. "So how should I adjust today's order?" — per-item recommended quantities come back as an order plan. "It's supposed to rain tomorrow — is that factored in?" — tomorrow is overcast, 0.0mm, so no rain adjustment was applied.

Across four follow-up questions the agent keeps the thread. You never re-explain the situation.

09:00 — Click the chart, and that becomes the question

Click the 11:00–13:00 peak bar on the hourly visitor chart, and the question "what sold well in that window?" is composed and sent automatically. Answer: pre- and post-meal demand — grab-and-go food, drinks, and energy pick-me-ups lead. The visualization itself is a question button.

11:30 — Just before the lunch peak: call in another person?

"How congested is the store right now?" — quiet overall (8 customers, average dwell 3.2 minutes). "The lunch-box corner?" — moderate (12), watch it for another 10–20 minutes. "Should I add someone at the register?" — 4 people there, no need yet.

Each zone has its own real headcount, with a grade (quiet · moderate · busy) and a recommended action.

15:00 — Afternoon sales review, and the weather

"What sells on rainy days?" — overall purchases drop 29.3% in the rain, but beer, glutinous-rice cakes, ice cups, and cup noodles hold up. When rain is forecast: trim regular grab-and-go, keep or slightly raise these.

17:30 — Shelf moves, with ROI attached

"Review my shelf layout." — activating the dead zone ranks first at an expected ROI of about ₩678K. "If I put snacks where the alcohol is?" — likely positive for snacks given bought-together patterns, but in a store with heavy alcohol sales, check whether the snack gain offsets the loss. A calculation only possible because CCTV pathways and POS run in one engine.

18:00 — Staffing retrospective

Mention "staff efficiency" and a dedicated analysis panel opens beside the chat. Deep metrics — counter coverage, shelf-zone management rate, counter gaps — live in the panel; the conversation helps you judge.

21:00 — Close, and tomorrow

"Wrap up today and tell me what to watch tomorrow." — today's summary comes with tomorrow's ordering direction (trim rice balls, nudge A-grade up). "Will tomorrow's briefing reflect all this automatically?" — "Yes. Tomorrow's briefing is recalculated on today's latest data."

The morning briefing flows into the evening close, and the close into tomorrow's briefing. And the loop gets better with use — a self-improvement pipeline turns the owner's feedback into the next fix.

AI recommends, people decide

Nine scenes, one constant: saai agent drafts the action plan, but a person always presses approve. The purchase order, the shelf move, the extra staffer — the AI advises; the owner decides.
